🧠 ONE PLACE THAT KNOWS YOUR PRODUCT

A shared brain for your whole team.

Product Brain is one place that holds what your team knows — your plans, your words, your decisions — and connects it to your apps. Anyone can ask a question in plain language and get a clear, trustworthy answer.

Think of it like a knowledgeable teammate who has read every document and every line of code, never forgets, and is happy to answer the same question a hundred times.

Three plain ideas

📚

One source of truth

All the important knowledge lives in one place, not scattered across chats, heads, and tools.

🔗

Connected to the code

It links your words and plans to the actual apps, so answers are real — not made up.

💬

Just ask

No special skills. Type a question like you'd ask a colleague, and get a clear answer.

You don't write specs a special way. Use whatever style your team already likes — plain notes, RFCs, exported docs. Product Brain organizes them; it doesn't boss you around.

The end of heavy technical documentation

Keeping thick technical documents up to date — and reading through them to find one answer — used to be a real burden. It isn't anymore. In a world with capable LLMs and indexing tools like graphify, you can simply ask a question or request an explanation and get it, grounded in the real code and decisions. You no longer have to write and maintain heavy docs just so people can find things, or dig through pages to get unstuck. The knowledge that is worth writing down — your rules, your words, your "why" — stays small and human; everything else, you just ask.

How it works

There's one button to remember: refresh. Everything else is just asking questions.

What "refresh" does

The refresh button is one command — pb sync — and you can just ask Claude to do it ("update me", "refresh the brain"). It first pulls everyone's latest hub changes (so you see teammates' new docs) and your apps' latest code, then a tool called graphify reads it all and rebuilds the map of how everything connects. After that, when you ask a question, Claude follows the map straight to the few relevant pieces — fast, and grounded in what's really there.

You run refresh after meaningful changes (or set it to run automatically). That's the whole maintenance routine.

Refreshing is fast after the first time

The first refresh does the most work. After that, graphify remembers what it has already read (it fingerprints each file), so a refresh only re-reads what actually changed. Routine refreshes are quick and cheap — which makes it easy to run often, or schedule nightly, so the brain is never stale.

It reads more than text

You don't have to turn everything into tidy documents. graphify can read code, written docs (Markdown, PDFs, Word, spreadsheets), images, and even meeting recordings — it transcribes audio and video on your own machine. So a recorded kickoff call can become searchable knowledge sitting right next to the code it affects.

Plain-language tip: "the graph" or "the map" just means a connected index of your stuff. You never edit it by hand — refresh rebuilds it for you.

Anatomy of a hub

A "hub" is just a folder (kept in Git) that holds your team's knowledge. Here's everything inside it, in plain terms.

acme-brain/                  ← the hub (one folder, its own Git repo)
  brain.config.json          settings: which apps to read, what doc types you keep
  constitution.md            your rules — the things that must always be true   [REQUIRED]
  vocabulary.md              your glossary — product words ↔ code words          [REQUIRED]
  domains.md                 the big areas of your product                       [recommended]
  docs/                      your knowledge, grouped by type
    specs/                     what you're building and why
    decisions/                 choices you made, and the trade-offs
    meeting-notes/             notes and recordings from meetings
    research/   runbooks/      ...add any type you want
  workflows/                 short "how I use this" guides, one per role
  graph/                     the map (built for you by refresh)
  .work/                     scratch space refresh uses (ignored by Git)

The two things you must have required

📜 constitution.md

Your non-negotiable rules — for example, "every user only ever sees their own data." If a change breaks one of these, it's wrong. Short and stable.

📖 vocabulary.md

Your shared words. Each entry says what a term means, what else people call it, and what it's named in the code. This is the bridge that ends "wait, what do you mean by that?"

These two matter most because they hold knowledge that can't be guessed from the code — your intent and your language.

Strongly suggested recommended

domains.md lists the major areas of your product (like "Scheduling" or "Billing") with an owner and status. It's "graph-assisted": after a refresh, the map already groups related things into clusters, so Claude can suggest the domains and you just tidy up the names. Less typing, same value.

Everything else is yours your choice

The docs/ folder is open-ended. Keep specs, decisions, meeting notes, research, runbooks — or invent your own type by adding it to brain.config.json. Write in Markdown when you can (it's simplest), but drop in PDFs or recordings whenever that's the natural form. You never have to link documents together by hand — the map connects related things automatically.

What each file is for, at a glance

brain.config.json
The only settings file. Lists your apps and the doc types you keep. Claude can edit it for you.
constitution.md
Rules every change must respect.
vocabulary.md
The glossary tying product words to code words.
domains.md
The product's big areas, owners, and status.
docs/<type>/
Your actual knowledge, grouped however you like.
workflows/<role>.md
A short "how I use the hub" note per role.
graph/
The map. Built by refresh — don't edit by hand.

The setup wizard

brainify is a wizard you talk to. It checks what's there, asks a few questions, and writes the files for you — no templates to wrestle with.

You start it by typing one thing to Claude inside your hub:

YouSet up product brain
Claude (brainify)Auditing this hub… Here's what's present and what's missing:
✅ brain.config.json · ❌ constitution · ❌ vocabulary · ❌ graph. Want to start at the top?
YouYes, let's go
ClaudeGreat. First, your rules. What are the 2–4 things that must always be true in this product? (For example: "users only see their own data.")
YouUsers only see their own data, and we never hard-delete anything.
ClaudeGot it — I've drafted constitution.md with those. Take a look; change anything you like. Next, let's capture your key words…

What the wizard does, step by step

  1. Audit — it looks at your hub and shows a simple checklist of what's there and what's missing.
  2. Interview — it asks plain questions, one at a time, and waits for your answers.
  3. Write docs — it drafts the files from your answers and shows them to you to approve or tweak.
  4. Build the map — it runs refresh so everything becomes searchable.
  5. Ask anything — you (and everyone else) can now ask questions.

You can also say "what should I do next?" any time, and the wizard suggests the single most useful next step.

Setup guide

About 15 minutes — and you can ask Claude to do every command for you.

Not technical? You're fine. You don't need to know git, Python, or how to install anything. Just ask Claude to do each step ("install the tools", "create the hub", "refresh") and it runs them and tells you what happened in plain words. The only step that might need a technical teammate, once, is creating the hub on GitHub and signing in the first time. Everything after setup is just asking questions — no commands at all.

The steps below show the commands for reference. If you're non-technical, read them as "things to ask Claude to do," not things you type.

1

Install the two tools

graphify builds the map; Claude (Cowork or Claude Code) runs the wizard and answers questions.

pip install graphifyy        # the map builder; no AI key needed for code
graphify --version
2

Add the brainify skill to Claude

This is the wizard. It ships in this project under skills/brainify/.

  • In Cowork: open Settings → Capabilities and add the brainify skill (or install the Product Brain plugin if your team publishes one).
  • In Claude Code: copy the skills/brainify/ folder into your project's skills directory (e.g. .claude/skills/brainify/), or point your plugin config at it.
  • Quick check: type /brainify or "set up product brain" — if Claude starts the audit, it's installed.
3

Create the hub

Make a new, empty Git repository for the hub (e.g. acme-brain). This is where your knowledge will live.

4

Run the wizard

Open the hub in Claude and say "Set up product brain." It walks you through declaring your apps, writing your rules and glossary, and choosing your doc types — one step at a time.

5

Refresh

/path/to/product-brain/bin/pb --hub . sync     # pull apps + build the map
pb sync --dry-run                               # preview without building

Tip: add bin/ to your PATH so you can just type pb sync.

6

(Optional) Make an app aware of the hub

Paste templates/app-repo-claude-md-snippet.md into an app repo's CLAUDE.md so Claude working inside that app automatically uses the hub's glossary, specs, and rules. It's the only thing you ever add to an app, it's optional, and nothing breaks without it.

Your application repos need no changes to get started — the hub pulls them. Step 6 is a nice-to-have.

Customize it — just ask Claude

The templates are starting points. You almost never edit them by hand — you tell Claude what you want in plain English, and it makes the change.

Things you can just say

💬 "Add a new doc type called retros."
Claude adds it to brain.config.json and creates the docs/retros/ folder.

💬 "Add a term to the glossary: a 'Reminder' is a scheduled nudge; in code it's NotificationJob."
Claude adds the entry to vocabulary.md in the right format.

💬 "Add a rule: every list endpoint in the API must be paginated."
Claude adds it to constitution.md under the API notes.

💬 "Write a spec for password reset, using our spec template."
Claude drafts docs/specs/00X-password-reset.md for you to review.

💬 "Turn this meeting recording into a note and save it."
Claude transcribes, summarizes the decisions, and files it under docs/meeting-notes/.

Editing a template's shape

If you want to change what a template looks like — say your specs should always have a "Rollout plan" section — just say so:

YouUpdate our spec template so every spec has a "Rollout plan" section near the end.
ClaudeDone — I added a "Rollout plan" section to templates/spec-template.md. Want me to add it to the existing specs too?

That's the whole idea: describe the outcome, let Claude do the editing. You stay in plain language; the files stay tidy.

How-tos by role

Everyone reads the same hub — they just ask different things. Here's a starting point per role.

🧑‍💼 Product Manager

Understand an area, write specs, keep the shared words honest.

  • "What does the Scheduling area do, and what specs cover it?"
  • "Draft a spec for guest checkout using our template."
  • "Add 'waitlist' to the glossary."

👩‍💻 Engineer (backend or frontend)

Get grounded on a feature before you build, and keep the brain honest after.

  • "How does login work in the API today?"
  • "What rules must I follow for new endpoints?"
  • After a rename: "Update the glossary — Appointment is now Booking."

🧪 QA

Turn specs into test plans and find the surface area of a change.

  • "List the acceptance criteria for the create-task spec."
  • "What parts of both apps does booking touch?"

🎨 Designer

Ground designs in how things actually work and what users said.

  • "Summarize the research we have on onboarding."
  • "What does the current checkout flow do, step by step?"

🌱 New hire

One guided path instead of a scavenger hunt.

  • Read the constitution → the domains → the glossary.
  • "Walk me through how the Tasks area works across both apps."

Each role can keep a short note in workflows/ so their go-to questions are written down for the next person.

Example: a to-do app

A complete, tiny hub for a two-app product — a web app and an API. It ships in this project under examples/todo-app/, so you can copy it and poke around.

todo-brain/
  brain.config.json          tracks todo-api + todo-web
  constitution.md            per-user data · validate inputs · soft delete · tests before merge
  vocabulary.md              Task · List · Due date   (product words ↔ code words)
  domains.md                 Tasks · Lists
  docs/
    specs/001-create-task.md
    decisions/ADR-001-soft-delete.md
    meeting-notes/2026-06-17-kickoff.md

The glossary bridges product and code

## Task
A single thing the user wants to get done.
- Also called: todo, item, card
- In code: `Task` (todo-api), `TaskItem` (todo-web)

One spec, both apps

The create-task spec lists the apps it touches and tags each check by side, so product, engineering, and QA all read the same page:

Create a task   ·   apps: todo-api, todo-web

✓ (api)  POST /tasks with a title → saves it for the current user
✓ (api)  empty title → friendly error, nothing saved
✓ (web)  press Enter → the task appears at the top instantly
✓ (web)  if it fails → show an inline error, keep what was typed

Decisions and meeting notes live in the same hub, so "why can't we permanently delete a task?" has a written answer (see ADR-001), and the reasoning from the kickoff isn't lost. Ask "how does creating a task work across both apps?" and the answer draws on the spec, the glossary, and both codebases at once.

Contribute

Product Brain gets better when teams use it and report back. You don't need to be an engineer to help — pick a role below.

Contribution roles

🧪 Dev Tester

Goal: run Product Brain on your stack and report what breaks.

What to test & report: Did pb sync build a graph for your languages? Did answers point at the right code? What was confusing, slow, or wrong? Which file types didn't get picked up?

🧑‍💼 PM Tester

Goal: validate the writing-and-asking workflow without touching code.

What to test & report: Did the wizard's questions make sense? Was the spec template a good fit? Could you get useful answers to real product questions?

🔍 Researcher

Goal: check whether we're using the best tools for each job.

What to test & report: Is graphify the best map-builder? Are there better ways to keep the map fresh, or to detect drift? Share findings and alternatives.

✍️ Template Author

Goal: improve a doc-type template (spec, decision, meeting note) or add a new one.

What to test & report: Which sections were missing or unused? Propose a clearer template and the kind of team it fits.

🌱 Onboarding Guide

Goal: make the new-hire path excellent.

What to test & report: Walk a real new hire through it. Where did they get stuck? What question did the hub fail to answer?

How to send it in

Keep it simple — one short write-up in this shape:

Problem you hit  →  what you changed (or wish changed)  →  what you tested it on  →  what you're unsure about

That format works whether you're filing a bug, proposing a template, or sharing research.

Open problems

The honest list of what we haven't solved yet. Each one is a great place to contribute.

1. "What breaks if I change this?" across apps

Today the hub can find related things across apps, but it can't yet say precisely "change this model and exactly these screens break." Real cross-app impact analysis is the biggest open prize.

2. Keeping the map fresh automatically

Right now someone runs refresh (or schedules it). Ideally each app rebuilds its part automatically when code merges, so the hub is never stale.

3. Catching glossary drift

When code is renamed, the glossary's "in code" notes can go out of date. A checker that flags stale entries after each refresh would keep the bridge trustworthy.

4. Pulling in non-Git sources

Lots of knowledge lives in Notion, Drive, or Slack. Bringing that in (as documents the hub can read) without manual copy-paste is an open need.

5. Knowing what to write next

Teams won't document everything. Help deciding which missing specs or decisions matter most — based on what people actually ask — would focus the effort.

6. Proving it helps

We need a simple way to measure whether the hub actually saves time and improves answers, so teams can see the payoff and keep investing.

FAQ

Do I need to be technical to use it?

No. Once it's set up, you ask questions in plain language. Setup needs someone who can run two commands, once.

Do I have to write specs a certain way?

No. Use any style — plain notes, RFCs, exported docs. Product Brain organizes what you write; it doesn't impose a method.

Does anything change in our app code?

No. The hub reads your apps; it doesn't add files to them. (You can optionally add one guidance note to an app — see the setup guide.)

Is our code sent anywhere?

Code is read on your own machine to build the map. Written docs and images use Claude to understand them; recordings are transcribed locally.

How often do I refresh?

After meaningful changes, or on a schedule. It only re-reads what changed, so repeat refreshes are quick.

Can it answer questions that span two apps?

Yes — finding and explaining across apps works today. Precise "what breaks where" across apps is still on the open-problems list.

Where do I start?

The Setup guide. Or copy examples/todo-app/ to see a finished hub first.
